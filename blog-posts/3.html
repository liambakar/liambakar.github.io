The Strategic Imperative of Sound

Published on August 21, 2025

Brain & Music

The sensation of "chills" or pleasure experienced when hearing a particularly moving piece of music is directly correlated with the release of dopamine. As the brain becomes familiar with a song, it can begin to release dopamine upon hearing just the first few notes, creating a Pavlovian anticipation of pleasure. This physiological conditioning provides a profound mechanism for cultivating a subconscious and durable connection between a person and their environment.

Beyond dopamine, music's influence on the brain is widespread. It activates the amygdala, triggering a wide range of emotions, including fear and pleasure. Moreover, the retrieval of memories through music can evoke feelings akin to those experienced during the initial events (hippocampus). Fast-paced music can increase pulse rate and blood pressure while slow music has the opposite effect. All this to say, music affects you.

Miss Fu in Chengdu

During a recent brunch at Cocodaq, an upscale Korean fusion restaurant in Manhattan, I was captivated by the harmonious blend of aural and visual elements. The curated music, lighting, and thoughtful design created a palpable atmosphere that elevated the dining experience. It reminded me of the intentional ambiance at Miss Fu in Chengdu in Toronto. Henry shared that he knows a professional 'mood setter' from Hong Kong who specializes in crafting playlists to define the feel of a space. This fascinated me.

Every time I've been to Miss Fu, I feel like I'm entering a night club. I can't lie, I love that feeling. Food, music, and good company are tied for the top most enjoyable aspects of my life.
